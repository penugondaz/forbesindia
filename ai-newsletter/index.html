Dear Reader,

"For my team, the cost of compute is far beyond the costs of the employees,” Bryan Catanzaro, vice president of applied deep learning at Nvidia recently told Axios. Microsoft has cancelled most of its Claude Code licences after the tool became so popular internally that costs spiralled. Uber's CTO told The Information in April the company had already burned through its entire 2026 AI coding tools budget in four months. The paradox at the heart of this AI moment is sharpening: The technology that was supposed to cut costs is, for now, escalating them.

By the way, this sharply contradicts what Sam Altman told Forbes India during an editor’s briefing in New Delhi: “It [AI] will absolutely be cheaper... The energy costs of inference today—per line of code—are already far, far lower than the energy costs of a person doing equivalent work.” He added, “…a person also requires a huge amount of energy over their lifetime to “train”—to grow, to run their body and brain for decades…”

The rest of this week's news fits neatly around that tension. Here’s everything you need to know from the AI world this week:

Photo by Vasily Fedosenko / Reuters

News reports suggest that Microsoft is cancelling most Claude Code licences after costs spiralled; Uber burned through its entire 2026 AI coding budget in just four months. A Gartner report warns that while token prices will fall by about 90 percent by 2030, agentic models consume far more tokens per task, and Goldman Sachs forecasts a 24X increase in token consumption by 2030.

China's secret weapon in the AI race: Cheap energy

Photo by Fred Dufour / AFP

While the US dominates the cutting-edge chips sector, China holds a decisive energy advantage. The country generates more than twice as much electricity as the US—a lead set to widen as it adds over six times more generation capacity over the next five years. A new 'East Data, West Computing' initiative is linking data centres directly to renewables in sparsely populated western regions. Meanwhile, US grid limitations caused a 50 percent quarter-on-quarter drop in new data centre projects at end of 2025. As Elon Musk put it at Davos: 'The limiting factor for AI deployment is fundamentally electrical power’.

Meta launches 'Meta One' AI subscriptions

Photo by Marcin Golba/NurPhoto via Getty Images

Meta is going all-in on subscriptions. Alongside globally rolling out Instagram Plus ($3.99), Facebook Plus ($3.99), and WhatsApp Plus ($2.99), the company is testing its first paid AI tiers: Meta One Plus at $7.99/month and Meta One Premium at $19.99/month, initially in Singapore, Guatemala, and Bolivia. The plans unlock greater compute for image/video generation and deeper reasoning. Meta AI remains free for casual users. Shares rose about 4 percent on the announcement as investors welcomed a non-ads revenue stream.

Salesforce beats estimates but outlook worries investors

Photo by Brendan McDermid / Reuters

Salesforce reported Q1 FY2027 revenue of $11.13 billion (up 13 percent y-o-y), beating consensus of $11.05 billion. Adjusted EPS of $3.88 crushed the $3.12 estimate. Its Agentforce platform grew ARR over 200 percent y-o-y. Yet shares remain down about 32 percent in 2026 as investors fear the 'SaaSpocalypse'—the risk that AI agents erode the seat-based licensing model on which Salesforce built its empire. Q2 guidance came in just below Wall Street expectations, adding to jitters.

YouTube rolls out automatic AI content detection and new labels

Photo: Shutterstock

YouTube is making two changes to its AI disclosure system. First, AI labels on photorealistic content are moving to a more prominent position: Directly below the video player for long-form, and as an overlay on Shorts. Second, YouTube is launching automatic AI detection: If a creator doesn't disclose AI use but the system detects significant photorealistic AI content, it will now apply a label automatically. Creators can contest incorrect labels in YouTube Studio, though content made with YouTube's own tools (Veo, Dream Screen) will always carry the label.
