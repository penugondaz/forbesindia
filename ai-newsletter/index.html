1️⃣ Anthropic and SpaceX team up on compute, and that changes things

Dario Amodei, CEO and co-founder of Anthropic; Photo by Michael M. Santiago / Getty Images North America / Getty Images via AFP

Anthropic has struck a deal with SpaceX for compute capacity, and the implications go well beyond a vendor arrangement. For Anthropic, more compute means faster iteration on Claude, higher usage ceilings for enterprise clients, and stronger positioning in the coding and agentic workflow space where the model wars are currently being fought. It's also a signal that the AI infrastructure game is being played at a scale that traditional cloud partnerships alone can't support.

But zoom out, and there's a more uncomfortable subplot here. SpaceX is Elon Musk's company. Musk, of course, is also the founder of xAI, a direct competitor to both Anthropic and OpenAI. He sued OpenAI's Sam Altman last year, alleging the company had abandoned its non-profit mission. The Musk-Altman feud has been loud and public. This deal puts Musk's infrastructure in the service of Anthropic— OpenAI's most credible rival—which is either a purely transactional arrangement or a pointed one, depending on how you read it. Either way, it adds another layer to an already complicated set of allegiances at the top of the AI industry.

2️⃣ SEBI and the US government are all paying attention, for the same reason

Photo by Sebastien Bozon / AFP

Anthropic's newly unveiled model Mythos had a busy week, and not in the way most product launches do.

In the US, the Department of Commerce's Center for AI Standards and Innovation announced that Microsoft, Google, and Elon Musk's xAI have agreed to give the government early access to their new AI models for national security testing—a pledge that stems from the Trump administration's AI Action Plan of July 2025. The driver, per officials: Growing alarm over the hacking capabilities of Mythos. The model's ability to identify system vulnerabilities at scale has put regulators on high alert.

In India, SEBI issued a parallel advisory this week, explicitly naming Mythos as an example of advanced AI tools that could expose financial institutions to heightened cybersecurity risk. The regulator has constituted a task force called cyber-suraksha.ai—bringing together market infrastructure institutions, registrar and transfer agents, and other regulated entities—to coordinate vulnerability management and information sharing across India's securities ecosystem. SEBI's concern: The deep interconnectedness of market participants means a single AI-exploited weakness could trigger cascading failures.

3️⃣ Sarvam and Pixxel are taking Indian AI to orbit

Pratyush Kumar, Co-Founder and CEO at Sarvam. Photo by Sebastien Bozon/ AFP India's sovereign AI ambitions just got an altitude upgrade.

India's sovereign AI ambitions just got an altitude upgrade.

Sarvam AI has announced a partnership with Bengaluru-based space tech startup Pixxel to power the AI backbone of what will be India's first orbital data centre satellite. The premise is straightforward but genuinely ambitious: India-built AI models will run on an India-built satellite, with both training and inference happening directly in orbit—without any dependence on foreign cloud or ground infrastructure.

What makes this technically interesting is the departure from how satellites have traditionally worked. Most satellites collect data, beam it back to Earth, and wait for ground systems to process it—a delay that is one of the biggest bottlenecks in Earth observation. The Sarvam-Pixxel mission cuts that loop entirely. The 200 kg-class satellite will carry data center-class GPUs in orbit, with Sarvam's models running on board to analyse Pixxel's hyperspectral imagery in real time as it is captured—flagging wildfires, tracking crop disease, or monitoring pipeline leaks in the same pass it observes them.

For Sarvam, this is an extension of its full-stack sovereign AI thesis beyond terrestrial infrastructure. For India, it's a proof-of-concept that the country can control its own intelligence pipeline from capture to insight—end to end, in space. The satellite has ambitious plans to reach orbit as early as Q4 2026.

4️⃣ SAG-AFTRA draws the line on AI clones

Photo by Shutterstock

Hollywood's actors' union has been fighting the AI replacement threat for a while now and this week, that effort came into sharper focus.

SAG-AFTRA—(Screen Actors Guild – American Federation of Television and Radio Artists), a major American labour union representing approximately 160,000–170,000 media professionals worldwide—has secured agreements requiring studios to obtain informed consent before digitally recreating any performer's voice or likeness, and to pay for AI-generated performances at rates equivalent to live human work. The union has also specified protections for voice cloning in video games, including a deal with Replica Studios.

The underlying logic of SAG-AFTRA's strategy is worth noting. Instead of trying to ban AI outright, the union is making it expensive enough that studios can't use it as a simple cost-cutting tool. That's a more durable protection—and a model other creative industries are watching closely.

5️⃣ ElevenLabs hits $500M ARR—with an unusual investor roster

Photo by Shutterstock

Audio AI company ElevenLabs has crossed $500 million in annual recurring revenue, announcing a fresh round that brings in a notably eclectic set of backers: Institutional investors including BlackRock and Wellington, enterprise players like NVIDIA and Santander, and celebrities including Jamie Foxx and Eva Longoria.

The ARR number is the real story. At $500M and growing, ElevenLabs has become one of the most commercially successful pure-play AI companies outside the foundation model tier. Voice and audio AI—synthetic narration, dubbing, agent voices—has found an enterprise product-market fit faster than many predicted, and ElevenLabs is the clearest proof point.

The celebrity investors are a footnote, but not an irrelevant one. In a category where consumer trust and brand perception matter (would you notice if a podcast was AI-voiced?), having recognisable names on the cap table is a soft-power play.

6️⃣ Meesho says 70% of its code is now AI-generated

Photo Illustration by Thomas Fuller/SOPA Images/LightRocket via Getty Images

SoftBank-backed e-commerce platform Meesho has become one of the most candid Indian tech companies about the pace of AI adoption inside its engineering teams. CEO Vidit Aatrey revealed this week that over 70 percent of the company's code is now AI-generated, as Meesho ramps up investment in AI-led commerce infrastructure.

That number deserves context. AI-assisted code generation—via tools like GitHub Copilot or Claude—has become standard across the industry, but 70 percent is a high watermark even by global standards. For Meesho, which built its business on cost efficiency and scale across Tier 2 and Tier 3 India, AI in engineering is less a prestige signal and more a structural bet on leaner, faster product development.
