When an AI model too dangerous to release publicly prompts a government-level emergency meeting with bank chiefs, something has shifted. That is what happened in India this week, after Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman convened heads of banks and Reserve Bank of India officials to discuss the risks posed by Anthropic's Claude Mythos—a model announced on April 7 that the company has declined to make publicly available. The concern is specific: Mythos has demonstrated an unprecedented ability to identify vulnerabilities in major operating systems, raising the prospect of AI-assisted cyberattacks at scale.

Anthropic is deploying it only under Project Glasswing, a controlled programme granting gated access to select organisations for defensive cybersecurity use. Banks were urged to take pre-emptive measures to protect their systems and customer data.

It is a striking moment. A model that never went public has already triggered an emergency policy response in the world's most populous country. And it lands against a broader backdrop that legal and policy experts in India have been flagging for months—autonomous AI agents are racing well ahead of the frameworks meant to govern them. As companies roll out agents across payments, banking, health care and supply chains, regulators are left without a dedicated legal regime for systems that can act autonomously, trigger other AI tools, and operate with little or no human oversight.

Existing laws covering contracts, liability, consumer protection and data governance are being stretched well beyond their original design. Particular unease surrounds agent-to-agent interactions. So, the question remains, who is responsible when automated systems fail? With oversight still grounded in high-level principles and voluntary guidelines, momentum is building for risk-based regulation and sandboxed experimentation.

That tension between capability, access and control shaped the AI agenda this week. Here are the key developments:

1️⃣ Amazon commits up to $25 billion to Anthropic in the week's biggest deal

Photo by Jakub Porzycki/NurPhoto via Getty Images

Amazon and Anthropic announced an expanded partnership on Monday that includes up to $25 billion in new investment and more than $100 billion in AWS cloud commitments over 10 years. Amazon is investing $5 billion immediately, with up to $20 billion more tied to commercial milestones—on top of the $8 billion it has already put into Anthropic since 2023.

The deal secures Anthropic access to up to 5 gigawatts of compute capacity, spanning Amazon's Trainium2 through Trainium4 chip generations. The full Claude Platform will be embedded natively within AWS, allowing enterprise customers to access it through their existing accounts without separate contracts or credentials.

The context is important. Two months prior, Amazon had struck a similarly structured arrangement with OpenAI—$50 billion in investment and a $100 billion cloud commitment. Amazon is now the dominant financial backer of both leading frontier labs simultaneously, positioning AWS as the infrastructure backbone of the frontier AI industry regardless of which lab wins the model race.

2️⃣ Indian IT turns AI ambition into commercial commitments

Photo by Shutterstock

The week offered a useful reality check on where Indian IT actually stands on AI—commercially, not just aspirationally. HCLTech reported annualised Advanced AI revenue of $620 million alongside an AI Factory deal worth over $100 million, meaningful commercial traction signals from a major Indian player. On the same day, the company launched a dedicated Gemini Enterprise Business Unit, formalising a large-scale services push around Google's agentic AI stack and signalling deeper enterprise packaging of AI transformation.

Infosys, meanwhile, announced a strategic collaboration with OpenAI to combine OpenAI products, including Codex, with Infosys Topaz, targeting enterprise software modernisation and agentic AI adoption at scale. These are not pilot programmes; they are commercial commitments with disclosed revenue figures.

3️⃣ OpenAI launches Workspace Agents in ChatGPT

Photo by Shutterstock

OpenAI launched workspace agents in ChatGPT this week—shared AI agents designed to handle complex tasks and extended workflows across tools and teams, operating under the permissions and governance rules defined by the organisation. Powered by Codex, the agents are built to run as persistent assistants that connect to third-party software, retain context across projects, and continue working even when users are offline.

Integrations at launch include Slack, Google Drive, Salesforce, Notion and Atlassian. Teams can create an agent by simply describing a task or uploading a file; ChatGPT then defines the steps, connects the tools and builds the workflow. Agents can also pause and seek human approval before taking consequential actions—a nod to the governance concerns that have followed agentic AI into the enterprise. The feature effectively replaces custom GPTs, which never achieved mainstream traction within ChatGPT. It is free until May 6, after which credit-based pricing begins.

4️⃣ Meta and Microsoft move to cut costs as the AI spending bill mounts

Photo by Shutterstock

The human cost of the AI infrastructure build-out came into focus on Thursday, when Meta and Microsoft announced workforce reductions totalling as many as 23,000 roles. Meta told employees it would cut 10 percent of its workforce—roughly 8,000 people—starting May 20, and would not fill 6,000 open roles. Microsoft, in a separate announcement, offered voluntary buyouts to about 7 percent of its US workforce, making approximately 8,750 employees eligible in what the company described as an unprecedented move at this scale. The buyout programme is aimed at employees whose age and years of service combined total 70 or more, as per news reports.

Both companies have been pouring billions into data centres and AI infrastructure. Big Tech firms are collectively projected to invest around $650 billion in AI infrastructure and data centres in 2026 alone. The cuts are, in effect, the other side of that ledger—headcount being rationalised to offset capital expenditure that shows no sign of slowing. Both companies report quarterly earnings on April 29.

5️⃣ DeepSeek returns with V4—and this time the numbers are larger

Photo by Shutterstock

DeepSeek announced preview versions of its new flagship model on Thursday, exactly a year after the Chinese startup's R1 model ignited a trillion-dollar stock market selloff and forced Silicon Valley to reckon with what a low-cost open-source model could do. This time, the Hangzhou-based company is going bigger.

The V4 series comprises two models: V4-Pro, with 1.6 trillion total parameters and 49 billion activated per token, and V4-Flash, a leaner 284 billion parameter variant designed for low-latency, cost-sensitive use cases. Both support a one-million-token context length and are released under the MIT licence, available on Hugging Face for research and commercial use. A key architectural innovation DeepSeek calls Hybrid Attention Architecture—combining Compressed Sparse Attention and Heavily Compressed Attention—dramatically improves long-context efficiency; in a one-million-token context, V4-Pro reportedly requires only 27 percent of the inference compute of its predecessor V3.2.

Early benchmark results put V4-Pro ahead of GPT-5.4 on competitive programming benchmarks, a domain where closed frontier models have historically held the lead. Whether those claims hold under independent evaluation remains to be seen. But the broader point is structural: A year after its R1 shock, DeepSeek is back and the open-source AI pressure on American labs has not gone away.

6️⃣ 75 percent of Google's new code is now AI-generated

Photo by Shutterstock

Buried in the Cloud Next keynote was this week's most striking data point. Sundar Pichai disclosed that 75 percent of all new code at Google is now AI-generated and approved by engineers, up from 50 percent last autumn—and from barely 30 percent in April 2025. A complex code migration, he said, was recently completed six times faster than was possible a year ago with engineers alone.

The trajectory has been near-vertical: From roughly a quarter of code in late 2024, to nearly half by the end of 2025, to three-quarters today. Pichai is deliberate in his framing, “AI is not replacing engineers,” but fundamentally redefining what engineering looks like. Human expertise is now concentrated at the review and direction stage rather than line-by-line composition. That Google, which writes some of the most complex production code in the world across Search, Ads, Cloud, YouTube and Android, has crossed this threshold is a meaningful signal about where the rest of the industry is heading.
