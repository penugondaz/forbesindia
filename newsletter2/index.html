The woman who brought down a President

Dear Reader,

Katharine Graham was not her father Eugene Meyer’s first choice to lead The Washington Post. That role went to her husband, Philip Graham, who inherited the paper from his father-in-law. When Philip, who suffered from bipolar disorder, died by suicide in 1963, Katharine stepped in as President.

By 1972, as CEO of The Washington Post Company, she had become the first female CEO of a Fortune 500 company. The same year her newspaper broke the Watergate scandal, triggering investigations into a political cover-up that ultimately forced President Richard Nixon to resign.

From Graham’s era to today’s boardrooms, this week we highlight the gender gap in India’s corporate leadership. While women hold nearly 1 in 3 board seats, legal mandates under the Companies Act explain the higher representation. Senior management, with no such requirement, lags at 17.1 percent though other management roles have edged up to 30 percent since 2019.

We are tracking two major shifts this week—the lead-up to high-stakes Assembly Election results across 5 states (including a Union Territory) and a shifting economic climate.

Before the Assembly Election results of West Bengal, Tamil Nadu, Assam, Kerala and Puducherry are out next week, check out our analysis on how the Special Intensive Revision drive is linked to the record voter turnout in West Bengal.

Meanwhile, the war in West Asia is beginning to exert tangible economic pressure. Latest industrial data shows the March IIP growth slowing to a 5-month low of 4.1 percent. Meanwhile, the Finance Ministry warns that high prices and cooling economic activity are triggering a concerning compression in demand.

Beyond the news, explore our exclusive features. Actor Sonali Bendre discusses her cancer journey, the healing power of her book club, and the film industry’s evolution. Plus, rewatch our conversation with Sachin Tendulkar on his mission to build a fitter India, republished during his birthday week.

Until next time!
-Samreen

 
Graphic of the Week Graphic of the day: Women at the top

India’s corporate boardrooms show higher female representation (29.1 percent) than senior management roles (17.1 percent) in 2025. While Boards are legally required to include women under the Companies Act, driving that number up, senior management roles, however, face no such mandate. As a result, women hold just 1 in 5 senior management roles but nearly 1 in 3 board seats. Moreover, female representation in other management roles has increased between 2019 and 2025 from 27 percent to almost 30 percent.
