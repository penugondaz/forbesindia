How does war change consumption in a household?

Dear Reader,

The attack on Pearl Harbor did more than just thrust the United States into a global conflict; it also triggered a shift in domestic life, significantly altering the consumption patterns of urban Americans.

As the nation pivoted to a war economy, urban spending on food climbed from 31 percent to 36 percent between 1941 and 1944, while expenditures on housing and transportation dropped. With factories prioritising the front lines, civilian goods like cars and appliances vanished from showrooms driving the household share of automobile spending down from 10 percent to just 4 percent, shows data from a war time survey by the US Labour Bureau.

But the one non-essential thing people did not give up on? Reading. This, even as urban households scaled back their education budgets.

Much like that wartime generation, today’s consumers find themselves at a crossroads of necessity and habit. So, this week we bring you an economy navigating war-linked contraction in production, fragile consumer sentiment and structural challenges in labour and health care; with technology posing as an opportunity and a risk.

That fragile resilience plays out on the factory floor as well—our ground report from the aftermath of a worker protest finds that wage hikes have quietened unrest in Uttar Pradesh’s manufacturing belt but left deeper labour grievances untouched.

Elsewhere, rural health insurance is expanding but out-of-pocket hospital costs have nearly doubled since 2017-18.

For your watchlist we bring you two stories: A tribute to Mark Mobius, the emerging markets legend who died at 89 and eerily foresaw pandemic-style global contagion risks in 2019; and a debate around Anthropic’s unreleased Mythos model, which can reportedly find and exploit software vulnerabilities at near-expert human level, raising fresh alarm about AI and cybersecurity.

Until next time!
-Samreen
