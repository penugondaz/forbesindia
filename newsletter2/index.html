Are India’s workplaces getting better at tackling sexual harassment?

Dear Reader,

A social worker from Rajasthan is the reason we have guidelines against workplace harassment in place today.

Bhanwari Devi tried to stop the marriage of a nine-month-old girl back in 1992 which resulted in a brutal sexual assault on her, an atrocity that eventually forced the legal system to act. Her case led to the formulation of the Vishaka guidelines, the critical precursor to the POSH Act that governs workplace conduct today.

However, decades after Devi’s struggle paved the way for these protections, the rise in POSH complaints across India’s listed companies is being met with a persistent resolution gap, creating a troubling trend that mirrors the delays of the recent sexual harassment case in Nashik.

This week we write about how workplace harassment complaints at listed firms rose 45 percent but resolution remains a challenge.

Closer to home, the effects of war are showing up in India’s numbers. India’s inflation held at 3.4 percent in March, driven by fuel price spikes and food costs. Trade deficit narrowed as crude imports fell 36 percent. Labour force participation hit a five-month low, with young women bearing the brunt of unemployment.

For your watchlist, we bring you the case at TCS Nashik that puts India’s largest IT firm under the spotlight.

In sports, badminton star Lakshya Sen opens up about resilience. As only the second Indian to reach the All England final twice, he reflects on overcoming a recent slump and sets his sights on gold at the 2028 Olympics.

Until next time!
-Samreen
