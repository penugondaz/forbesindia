Hello and welcome back to Forbes India Weekly Digest!

India welcomed US Secretary of State, Marco Rubio last week. Apart from discussing bilateral cooperation across key sectors like defence, energy security, trade and investment, among others, he announced the new ‘America First’ visa scheduling framework.

The framework prioritises appointments for business professionals like corporate executives, entrepreneurs, and investors traveling between US and India for economic and strategic interests.

The announcement comes at a time when registrations for H-1B visa to the US dropped by 38.5 percent for FY27 compared to the previous year, reflecting the changes in visa policies introduced by President Donald Trump to restrict outsourcing.

The rules which came into effect in September 2025 included a one-time $100,000 sponsorship fee to be paid by the employers for each petition under the H1B visa category and weighted system of approval for higher salaried individuals.

All of this, in addition to Trump administration’s latest rule which mandates people applying for permanent residency in the US or green card, to apply from their home country except in extraordinary circumstances makes it difficult for Indian students, researchers, medical and tech professionals working there.

Indians typically make up for nearly 70 percent of approved H1B applications – with many opting for the status of an immigrant by applying for an ‘adjustment of status’ while living in the US.

The ongoing layoffs by large tech companies like Meta, Oracle and others jeopardises work and family life for Indians on H1B visas further. Time for a reality check on the American dream!

👷Gig workers stumped by working hours and income protection hurdles

India’s gig and platform workers finally became a part of the Indian social security network earlier this month, thanks to the rules notified under the Code on Social Security, 2020. However, ambiguity on who is eligible for benefits and compliance cost for companies remains a major hurdle. 

➤As part of the rules, a worker must complete 90 days with a single aggregator or 120 days across multiple aggregators in a given financial year to qualify for benefits.

➤Compliance burden on companies employing gig or platform workers is complex if hired through third-party services like fleet owners, local vendors or staffing intermediaries.

➤If a worker’s account gets blocked midway, it impacts both income and their eligibility for the benefits.
