Hello and welcome back to Forbes India Weekly Digest!

If revisiting the past were a week, this would be it. The week took us back to the start of the decade, which was six years ago.

To begin with, the Indian government has decided to ease the Covid-era decision to restrict the inflow of Chinese capital to India.

Introduced via Press Note 3 in April 2020, Indian government had mandated pre-approval for investments by countries sharing a land border with India, over concerns of opportunistic takeovers.

Earlier this week, the union cabinet decided to ease the restrictions to encourage non-controlling investments under the automatic route to encourage sectors reliant on Foreign Direct Investment, such as technology, manufacturing, and startups. This becomes significant at a time of mounting tariffs from the US government and its direct effect on manufacturing sectors relying heavily on exports. Despite the low FDI, China is India’s second largest trading partner.

Inflation data too took us back in time as retail inflation rose to 3.21 percent in February compared to 2.74 percent in January, due to rising food prices. And for those of us who thought it was normal to anticipate and pre-book LPG cylinders, those days are back again, thanks to the ongoing war in West Asia.

And if you needed further proof, US-based General Motors is back in India, not with automobiles but with its automotive parts business.

🍵Restaurants feel the brunt of LPG shortage

Ordering in because you are trying to conserve LPG at home might not be an option as restaurants too are facing an acute shortage of commercial LPG cylinders due to the US-Israel attacks on Iran. Restaurants across Indian metros including Bengaluru, Chennai, Delhi, Mumbai and Pune are feeling the heat due to delayed delivery and uncertain supply of LPG cylinders.

➤India’s restaurant industry contributes more than Rs 5 trillion in revenue to the economy every year, employing 8 million people.

➤Restaurants are currently taking items off their menu which require longer cooking time.

➤India is the second-largest importer of LPG in the world and more than 50 percent of its requirements are met through imports from Gulf Countries.

🔑General Motors makes a comeback to India

Iconic American automotive maker General Motors is back in India again. This time, it is not selling cars but automotive parts instead. The company has brought back its global aftermarket brand ACDelco to India through a licensing partnership with Assurance International Limited.

➤GM exited India in 2017 failing to capture market share with its brand Chevrolet.

➤ACDelco shut its operations in 2020. As part of its re-entry, it will make branded batteries and lubricants for India.

➤ACDelco has invested Rs 180 crore in its lubricant blending and packaging plant, with Rs 300 crore outlay for battery manufacturing.

🚢The Hormuz effect on corporate India

After battling weak consumption and volatile input costs, corporate India is now bracing for the impact from the conflict in Iran. The structural damage to global trade routes and energy pricing is likely to impact Q4 margins of publicly listed companies in India, say experts.

➤Nearly 34 percent of listed company expenditure goes towards power and fuel – impacted by the ongoing conflict.

➤Crude-linked sectors such as paints, tyres and speciality chemicals are likely to experience maximum margin squeeze.

➤If the war continues for long, the Strait of Hormuz being at high-risk will act as a drag on Indian exports and corporate margins.
