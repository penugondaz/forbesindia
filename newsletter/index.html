Hello and welcome back to Forbes India Weekly Digest!

The war against Iran by US and Israel has been on for over a week now, and there seems to be no respite.

The continued escalation in West Asia has cost US nearly $900 million a day, according to latest research. The Pentagon would need more money to cover the unbudgeted costs, according to reports, adding to the anti-war sentiment amid rising oil prices and persistent inflation.

The unrest hasn’t spared India as it has choked the country’s cheapest oil routes and has cost India’s aviation sector Rs 200 crore per day due to airspace closure. Surprisingly, gold remained muted while Rupee crashed and central bank had to step in to protect the currency.

Amidst the talk of all the war, India’s consumption graph looks promising with demand picking up across automobiles, smartphones, consumer appliances, and housing, especially in the urban markets.

The impact of the war will colour everything for some time to come, here is the latest from the week gone by:

⛽Rising oil prices test economic resilience

The unrest in West Asia is squeezing all oil-importing nations, as the critical oil corridor of Strait of Hormuz sits at the centre of the escalating tensions. India’s move to wind down purchase of Russian oil as part of its recent trade agreement with the US complicates matters further, given the country’s reliance on crude imports. Analysts believe crude prices will soon soar to $90 per barrel — a prediction which came true this week.

🔹Nearly 40 percent of India’s crude imports and 20 percent of global oil and LNG move through the Strait of Hormuz which is at the center of the crisis.

🔹India faces heightened risk as it imports nearly 85 percent of crude requirements.

🔹India has received a 30-day waiver from the US government to buy Russian oil currently stranded at sea.

🍿Urban consumption makes a comeback

Urban demand for consumer goods, automobiles, and housing has returned with easing inflationary pressures, easy credit, and GST rate rationalisation. However, consumer spending across the country remains uneven due to disruption of rural employment guarantee program.

🔹Automobile sector has seen sales of passenger vehicles rise by 26 percent over the last year for the month of February 2026.

🔹The growth in smartphone sales online has grown 15 to 20 percent year-on-year, leaning towards premium devices.

🔹The recovery cycle will depend on the durability of urban demand and pace of rural income growth.

🚙Dude, where’s my car?

GST rate cut for the auto sector has led to demand outstripping supply. India’s largest carmaker, Maruti Suzuki has resorted to working on Sundays and holidays to keep pace with the demand, with the situation likely to improve by May.

🔹Bookings are growing by nearly 20 percent, said a senior executive at the automaker.

🔹The plant is currently operating at over 100 percent capacity to keep up with the backlog of nearly 1.9 lakh units.

🔹The new production line opening in Maruti’s Kharkhoda plant in April will add 1.2 lakh units, easing the pressure from May onwards.
